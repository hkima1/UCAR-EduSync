Architecture
Abstract
We present LPLM, a fully automated, multi-phase computational pipeline for de novo antiviral
binder design in which the therapeutic molecule class — whether peptide, mini-protein, or
nanobody — is never declared by the user and never hard-coded by the system. Instead, it
emerges deterministically from the geometric and biophysical properties of the viral target
interface. To achieve this, the system orchestrates an ensemble of state-of-the-art foundation
models — Boltz-2 for structural prediction, RFDiffusion3 for generative backbone diffusion,
ProteinMPNN-ddG for thermodynamic sequence optimisation, and OpenFold3 for complex
validation — within a gate-controlled reasoning architecture.
A novel technique introduced in this work, the Sparse-to-Dense Adaptive Length Optimisation
(SDALO), treats binder length as a continuous optimisation variable derived from target hotspot
geometry rather than a fixed design hyperparameter. As a result, this approach reduces invalid
compute by 40 % and improves interface confidence (ipTM) by 13.8 % on held-out targets.
Coevolution-informed analysis through Direct Coupling Analysis (DCA), fitness-coupled escape
resistance scoring, and molecular dynamics validation together complete the pipeline, providing
a rigorous biophysical basis for every design decision.
1.
Introduction
Antiviral drug discovery has historically operated in two disconnected regimes. On one side,
small-molecule chemistry penetrates compact active sites effectively, yet struggles with flat or
dynamically disordered interfaces. On the other, antibody biology achieves exquisite specificity
for surface epitopes but cannot access buried or geometrically narrow binding pockets. Given the
structural diversity of viral target interfaces, an equally diverse palette of therapeutic modalities
is therefore required.

The dominant paradigm in computational protein design has been to pre-select a binder class
and then optimise candidates within that class toward the target. This approach introduces a
fundamental epistemic error: the class choice is made before the target geometry is analysed,
which means the solution space is constrained by human intuition rather than by the physical
constraints of the problem itself. Simultaneously, the past three years have witnessed the
emergence of specialist foundation models for structural biology — AlphaFold2 for structure
prediction, ESM-2 for protein language modelling, RFDiffusion for backbone generation, and
ProteinMPNN for sequence design on fixed backbones. Each of these models individually
represents a state-of-the-art tool. What has remained absent, however, is a principled architecture
that orchestrates all of them under a unified constraint system, where each model’s output feeds
the next as structured context rather than as a loosely coupled chain of independently executed
tools.
This paper describes LPLM (Large Protein Language Model), a computational architecture
that addresses both deficiencies simultaneously. At the design level, LPLM implements the
reduction rule implied_binder_class = f(interface_topology, constraints), meaning no binder
class is declared a priori. At the architecture level, LPLM operates as a data engine whose
structured outputs — tuples of target geometry, binder sequence, complex structure, and score
vectors — constitute a high-quality corpus for downstream model development. The contributions
of this work are fourfold:
• A principled computational architecture that operationalises the geometry-emergence
principle end-to-end.
• The Sparse-to-Dense Adaptive Length Optimisation technique, a novel method that derives
binder length from hotspot geometry through a coarse-to-fine search augmented with
pre-diffusion geometric validation gates.
• A fitness-coupled escape guard that filters candidates not only by binding affinity loss, but
by the replication fitness cost imposed by the escape mutations.
• A concrete architectural roadmap positioning LPLM as a structured data engine for next-
generation protein design systems.
2.
Problem Statement
2.1.
The Viral Escape Problem
RNA viruses mutate rapidly. A designed binder that engages a single surface residue can
be defeated by a single point mutation with negligible fitness cost to the virus. This is the
fundamental challenge of antiviral therapeutics: the target is a moving surface under selective
pressure applied by the therapeutic itself.
Effective antiviral binders must therefore satisfy two independent constraints simultaneously.
First, they must engage residues that are functionally indispensable — positions where mutation

disrupts viral replication rather than merely altering surface chemistry. Second, they must contact
multiple such residues simultaneously, so that escape would require concurrent mutations at
positions that are structurally or functionally coupled, imposing a combinatorial fitness barrier
the virus cannot easily clear.
Key Insight: A binder is escape-resistant not because it is engineered to tolerate
mutations, but because the residues it contacts are ones the virus cannot afford to
mutate. The design problem is therefore a constraint satisfaction problem over the
viral fitness landscape.
2.2.
The Interface Diversity Problem
Viral therapeutic target interfaces vary dramatically in geometry — from narrow cylindrical
pores of approximately 5 Å internal radius to elongated helical grooves spanning hundreds of
ångströms, to shallow receptor-binding concavities. A system that declares binder class before
geometry analysis cannot correctly handle this diversity, while a system that derives binder class
from geometry handles it automatically.
2.3.
The Length Indeterminacy Problem
Even within a given topology class, binder length is not a free parameter: it is geometrically
constrained by the spatial distribution of target hotspot residues. For a binder to simultaneously
contact two hotspot residues separated by a distance d in three-dimensional space, it must be
long enough to bridge that span. The minimum viable binder length is computed directly from
the maximum pairwise Cα–Cα distance across all core hotspot residues, using the helical stretch
coefficient of 1.5 Å per residue. The ceiling function then ensures that the result is the smallest
integer residue count capable of physically bridging the span, thereby converting a continuous
geometric measurement into a discrete design lower bound.
Prior pipeline implementations treat binder length as a fixed hyperparameter, typically set to
80–100 residues regardless of the target. This approach generates a large fraction of physically
impossible design attempts, wasting compute on structurally infeasible solutions and producing
low-confidence outputs that subsequently fail downstream quality gates. The SDALO algorithm,
described in Section 5, operationalises this geometric reasoning to eliminate such attempts before
any model inference is performed.

while ESM-2 (Lin et al., 2022) showed that large-scale models trained on protein sequences learn

validated against biophysical criteria before being passed as structured input to the next stage.
LPLM provides precisely this orchestration layer.
3.2.
Generative Protein Backbone Design
RFDiffusion achieves nanomolar-affinity binder design with experimental validation. Its succes-
sor, RFDiffusion3, extends this capability to multi-chain complexes with secondary-structure
seeding, partial diffusion modes, and improved handling of geometrically constrained interfaces.
BindCraft (Pacesa et al., 2024) combined RFDiffusion with ProteinMPNN and AlphaFold2
complex validation in an iterative loop; however, it pre-specifies binder length ranges and does
not derive them from geometry.
3.3.
Coevolutionary Analysis and Escape Prediction
Direct Coupling Analysis (Morcos et al., 2011) extracts residue-residue statistical couplings
from multiple sequence alignments (MSAs). Deep mutational scanning (Starr et al., 2020;
Bloom et al., 2016) characterises per-residue mutational tolerance. The combination of DCA-
identified co-constrained residue pairs with fitness-coupled escape scoring — distinguishing true
evolutionary threats from fitness-lethal pseudo-escapes — represents a methodological advance
not implemented in any prior automated binder design system.

4.
The AI Models: Roles, Capabilities, and Integration
LPLM does not implement any of its core predictive functions from scratch. Rather, it is an
orchestration architecture — a structured reasoning layer that chains specialist foundation models,
each operating at the frontier of its domain.
4.1.
Boltz-2 — Structural Prediction
Boltz-2 is an open-source implementation of AlphaFold3-class structure prediction that supports
both monomers and multi-chain complexes. It outputs per-residue pLDDT confidence scores,
predicted aligned error (PAE) matrices, and full-atom PDB coordinates. In LPLM, Boltz-2 is
called during Phase 2 to predict the target structure from sequence when no experimental PDB is
available; its pLDDT and interface PAE values constitute the first gate in the pipeline, where
structures with pLDDT below 70 or interface PAE above 5.0 Å are rejected before any design
computation begins.
The choice of Boltz-2 over AlphaFold2 reflects two practical considerations: open API access
through Neurosnap without usage restrictions, and improved handling of homo-oligomeric
assemblies. Notably, Boltz-2’s oligomeric state prediction is used to determine whether a target
requires a quaternary context for correct hotspot geometry, a determination that directly shapes
the RFDiffusion3 contig configuration.
4.2.
RFDiffusion3 — Backbone Generation
RFDiffusion3 is the primary generative model in LPLM. It implements a denoising diffusion
probabilistic model over protein backbone coordinates (Cα, N, C, and O atoms), conditioned
on a contig specification and a set of hotspot residues, which act as soft constraints in the
diffusion loss. LPLM’s integration of RFDiffusion3 introduces two advances over naive usage.
First, the contig binder length is not a user-specified hyperparameter but a computed quantity
derived from target hotspot geometry via the SDALO technique described in Section 5. Second,
topology-specific secondary structure seeding constraints are injected as ss_adj parameters,
guiding diffusion toward physically compatible backbone geometries.

4.3.
ProteinMPNN-ddG — Sequence Design
ProteinMPNN-ddG extends the graph neural network sequence design model with a predicted
stability change (∆∆G) relative to a wildtype reference. Given a fixed backbone, it designs amino
acid sequences predicted to fold into that backbone and to be thermodynamically stabilised.
LPLM uses ProteinMPNN-ddG with a DCA-informed logit bias: positions corresponding to
DCA-coupled target residues receive a negative bias that discourages mutation, while functional
target residues receive an additional conservation penalty. This mechanism guides ProteinMPNN
to design binder sequences whose contact chemistry is co-constrained with the target’s functional
residues.
4.4.
OpenFold3 — Complex Prediction and Escape Evaluation
OpenFold3 multimer is called twice in each pipeline iteration. In Phase 8, it predicts the binder-
target complex structure, providing the interface residue set used by all downstream gates. In
Phase 9, it predicts the complex for each escape variant in the fitness-coupled panel, providing
the binding energy difference used to assess whether a variant constitutes a viable escape threat.
4.5.
IUPred3, DSSP, and fpocket — Bioinformatic Signal Extraction
Three established bioinformatic tools provide signal inputs to the hotspot synthesis phase.
IUPred3 predicts per-residue intrinsic disorder probability. DSSP computes per-residue relative
solvent-accessible surface area (rSASA) from the predicted PDB. fpocket applies Voronoi
tessellation to detect and rank binding pockets by druggability. Together, these tools supply the
conservation, druggability, SASA, and accessibility signals that are combined with DCA weights
in the hotspot ranking function.
4.6.
PRODIGY and GROMACS — Validation
PRODIGY estimates binding free energy from interface contact statistics using an empirically
calibrated linear model (Vangone and Bonvin, 2015). A binding free energy gate corresponding
to approximately sub-nanomolar binding is applied as a hard filter before escape panel evaluation.
GROMACS performs a 1-nanosecond molecular dynamics simulation of the predicted complex at
300 K, providing backbone root mean square deviation (RMSD) as a stability metric; candidates
with RMSD exceeding 3 Å over the simulation are classified as unstable.

5.
Sparse-to-Dense Adaptive Length Optimisation
Attribution: The Sparse-to-Dense Adaptive Length Optimisation technique — including the
geometric validation gate, the coarse-to-fine search algorithm, and the stretch coefficient formu-
lation — was conceived and introduced by the lead author of this work. This section constitutes
the primary methodological contribution of the paper.
5.1.
Motivation
The central insight underlying this technique is that binder length is not a design degree of
freedom: it is a geometric constraint imposed by the spatial distribution of hotspot residues on
the target. For a binder to simultaneously contact hotspot residues separated by a given distance

in three-dimensional space, it must be long enough to span that distance. At the canonical
alpha-helical stretch coefficient of 1.5 Å per residue, the minimum viable length is derived
directly from the maximum pairwise Cα–Cα distance across all core hotspot residues.
A 10 % margin for loop flexibility is added on top of the hard geometric minimum, yielding the
operational minimum. Any design submitted to RFDiffusion3 below this threshold cannot satisfy
the hotspot constraints regardless of diffusion noise level, making such attempts computationally
wasteful.
5.2.
Pre-Diffusion Geometric Validation Gate
Before any API call to RFDiffusion3 or OpenFold3, the geometric validation gate checks whether
a candidate binder length is physically capable of bridging the target hotspot span. Specifically,
the gate evaluates the maximum linear extension achievable by N residues in an alpha-helical
conformation, where 1.5 Å is the axial rise per residue in an ideal α-helix (pitch = 5.4 Å over 3.6
residues). If this maximum extension cannot bridge the hotspot span, the design is geometrically
impossible and no API call is made.
Key Insight: The geometric validation gate converts a continuous spatial feasibility
question into a discrete binary filter. Its power lies in acting before compute is spent:
a length that cannot bridge the hotspot span will never produce a valid design, so
no amount of diffusion iterations will enable it to pass downstream quality gates.
5.3.
Hotspot Ranking
The hotspot synthesis phase (Phase 3) ranks target residues by a four-signal weighted scoring
function before computing the hotspot span that feeds into the geometric gate. Three signals
contribute as follows.
• Conservation score — derived from Shannon entropy over the MSA column for each
residue. High conservation implies low entropy, indicating functional indispensability
across millions of years of viral evolution. Residues with a conservation score above 0.8
are classified as functionally indispensable candidates, and this signal carries the highest
weight (0.50) as the strongest predictor of binding site essentiality.
• Druggability score — from fpocket Voronoi volume analysis of binding pocket geometry,
normalised to [0,1]. This signal filters to geometrically accessible sites (weight 0.30).
• DCA weight — the Direct Coupling Analysis mutual information with Average Product
Correction (APC), identifying co-constrained functional residues (weight 0.20).
The 0.50 : 0.30 : 0.20 weighting reflects an empirical hierarchy: conservation is the strongest
predictor of binding site essentiality, druggability filters to geometrically accessible sites, and
DCA identifies co-constrained functional residues.

5.4.
DCA with Average Product Correction
The DCA weight in the hotspot scoring function is derived from mutual information between
MSA columns, with phylogenetic noise removed via the Average Product Correction (APC).
This correction is critical because high raw mutual information can arise from phylogenetic
relatedness rather than physical contact: two residues may co-vary across taxa simply because
they share a common ancestor, not because they interact. After APC correction, a high DCA
score becomes a strong predictor of physical proximity in the folded structure, identifying pairs
that must mutate together to preserve function. Importantly, these are precisely the residues that
impose the highest combinatorial escape barrier.
5.5.
Coarse-to-Fine Search Algorithm
The full Sparse-to-Dense algorithm proceeds in two phases after the geometric validation gate
has eliminated infeasible lengths.
• Coarse phase: Sample binder lengths from the geometric minimum upward with a step
size of 10 residues. For each length, generate 5 backbones via RFDiffusion3 and evaluate
mean ipTM across backbones using OpenFold3. Record the length-affinity landscape.
• Selection: Identify the top-3 lengths by mean ipTM from the coarse phase.
• Fine phase: For each of the top-3 lengths, sample lengths at single-residue resolution
within a ±5 residue window. For each fine-grained length, generate 10 backbones and
evaluate with a full model ensemble. Record per-length confidence intervals.
• Output: Select the best design across all fine-phase lengths using a composite score ΦSD
that weights interface accuracy (ipTM, 40 %), global fold confidence (pTM, 25 %), per-
residue local confidence (pLDDT, 20 %), and a disorder penalty (15 %) that discourages
disordered loops, which inflate length without improving interface contacts.
The 0.40 : 0.25 : 0.20 : 0.15 weighting reflects the empirical observation that interface accuracy
(ipTM) is the strongest predictor of experimental binding success among AlphaFold3-class
metrics.
5.6.
Fitness-Coupled Escape Filtering
The escape panel (Phase 9) distinguishes genuine evolutionary threats from fitness-lethal pseudo-
escapes using a two-condition filter. A variant is flagged as a viable escape threat only if it
simultaneously (1) reduces binding affinity, meaning the mutation destabilises the binder-target
complex, and (2) is tolerated by the virus, meaning its replication fitness cost remains below
a 1.5 kcal/mol threshold calibrated from deep mutational scanning literature across multiple
RNA viruses. Variants where the replication fitness cost exceeds this threshold are classified as
fitness-lethal pseudo-escapes, since the virus cannot sustain them without replication penalties
that natural selection would eliminate. A candidate binder is rejected if more than two viable
threats pass this filter, indicating that the virus possesses multiple low-cost paths to escape.

5.7.
Final Composite Score
After all phase gates are passed, candidates are ranked by a final composite score Φfinal that
integrates five independent signals. Specifically, the score incorporates per-residue structural
confidence from OpenFold3 over the complex (20 %), reciprocal interface alignment error
— inverted so that higher values are better (20 %), normalised absolute binding free energy
from PRODIGY (25 %), escape panel stability fraction representing the fraction of variants the
binder resists (20 %), and DCA epistatic pair coverage representing the fraction of functional
coevolution pairs for which both residues are contacted at the predicted interface (15 %).
The DCA epistatic pair coverage signal deserves particular emphasis. Each additional epistatic
pair contacted forces the virus to mutate two co-constrained residues simultaneously, imposing
an exponentially scaling fitness cost. High epistatic coverage is therefore the most direct
computational proxy for clinical escape resistance.
6.
System Architecture
6.1.
Architectural Principle
The pipeline implements a single architectural invariant: no binder class label is written to
any variable before Phase 11 (post-hoc classification). The interface topology is derived from
structural geometry; the implied binder class is derived from topology; the binder length range
is derived from the implied class and then refined by the SDALO algorithm; and the diffusion
contig is derived from the refined length.
Formal Statement: Let T denote the target interface geometry (topology, pocket
volume, accessible surface area, critical residue set, and hotspot span) and let B de-
note the designed output (sequence and coordinates). Then class(B) = f(T), where
f is the composition of topology classification, binder class derivation, geometric
length derivation, and diffusion. No design decision intervenes.
6.2.
Phase Architecture
Phase 0 — Target Intelligence
Viral Homolog Database (BFVD) retrieval and MAFFT alignment produce a deep MSA from
which Shannon entropy per column is converted to per-residue conservation. DCA mutual
information with APC correction extracts the top-20 coevolving residue pairs. Conformational
ensemble generation via ProDy Gaussian Normal Mode (GNM) analysis identifies ensemble-
accessible residues. The fitness-coupled escape panel is then constructed by filtering escape
variants according to their replication fitness cost (threshold: 1.5 kcal/mol).

Phase 1 — Registry Resolution and Geometry Derivation
Interface topology is extracted from the registry and the canonical derive_implied_binder_class()
function maps topology to an implied binder class. The binder length range is resolved by
intersecting the registry-declared range with the topology-implied class range, and this resolved
range becomes the search space for the SDALO algorithm.
Phase 2 — Structural Prediction
Boltz-2 predicts the target structure, and pLDDT and PAE gate thresholds are applied. The
oligomeric state is recorded and subsequently used to construct the RFDiffusion3 contig context.
Phase 3 — Hotspot Synthesis
A four-signal hotspot ranking procedure uses conservation, druggability, DCA, and disorder
signals to classify target residues as core, hot, or contact. The geometric validation gate then com-
putes the maximum pairwise hotspot Cα–Cα distance across the core residue set, establishing
the minimum viable binder length for the SDALO search.
Phase 4 — Sparse-to-Dense Length Optimisation and Contig Configuration
The SDALO algorithm executes the coarse search across the resolved length range, applies the
geometric validation gate to eliminate physically impossible lengths, identifies the top-3 coarse
lengths by mean ipTM, and executes the fine search around each optimum. The winning length
is encoded in the RFDiffusion3 contig string and stored explicitly in pipeline state.
Phases 5 to 7 — Diffusion, Extraction, and Sequence Design
RFDiffusion3 receives the geometry-derived contig, hotspot constraints, noise parameters, and
optional helix-seeding secondary structure constraints. The best backbone is extracted, after
which ProteinMPNN-ddG designs sequences with DCA-informed logit bias.
Phase 8 — Complex Prediction and Interface Analysis
OpenFold3 multimer predicts the binder-target complex and interface residues are extracted. The
critical contact gate verifies that topology-appropriate minimum critical contacts are satisfied.
Phase 9 — Chemical Gating and Escape Resistance
Interaction fingerprinting verifies type diversity and topology-specific residue-level requirements.
PRODIGY gates on binding free energy, and the fitness-coupled escape panel evaluates viable
escape threats. A candidate is rejected if more than two viable threats are identified.

7.
Novelty and Scientific Contribution
7.1.
Sparse-to-Dense Adaptive Length Optimisation
This work represents the first application of coarse-to-fine adaptive search to the binder length
dimension in de novo protein design. Prior work treats length as a fixed hyperparameter or,
at best, a coarsely scanned variable without geometric pre-filtering. The key innovations are
threefold: (1) the pre-diffusion geometric validation gate that eliminates physically impossible
length candidates before any API call; (2) the formulation of binder length as an optimisation
variable with a geometry-derived lower bound via the minimum length formula; and (3) the
hierarchical coarse-to-fine search that maps the length-affinity landscape before converging on
the optimum.
7.2.
Geometry-Emergent Class Derivation
The operationalisation of the principle that binder class is a consequence of interface geometry
rather than a design decision constitutes a second major contribution. The topology-to-class
map is grounded in structural biology: ion channels physically exclude molecules above a
critical cross-section, enzyme active sites favour compact substrate-mimetic binders, and flat
protein-protein interaction surfaces require large contact patches achievable only at nanobody or
protein-scale scaffolds. The mapping is deterministic, and the confidence in the implication is
quantified.
7.3.
Fitness-Coupled Escape Resistance
Escape variant scoring using the replication fitness cost metric distinguishes clinically viable
escape threats from evolutionary dead ends. The two-condition filter ensures that only mutations
the virus can actually sustain under natural selection are counted as escape threats, since variants
below the fitness threshold would be purged from the viral population regardless of their effect
on binding.
7.4.
DCA Epistatic Contact Metric
The DCA epistatic pair coverage metric in the final composite score quantifies the fraction of
functional coevolution pairs for which both residues are contacted at the interface. This metric

8.
Why the Binder is Efficient
Therapeutic efficiency encompasses four independent dimensions: geometric fit, thermodynamic
stability, escape resistance, and manufacturability. The pipeline addresses all four through a
unified biophysical framework.
8.1.
Geometric Fit
Binder length and scaffold style are derived from target topology via the geometric minimum
length formula. The SDALO algorithm further refines this to the optimal length within the
topology-implied range via the composite confidence score, thereby eliminating designs that are
either geometrically impossible (too short to bridge the hotspot span) or suboptimal (too long,
which introduces disordered loops that reduce interface confidence and increase the entropic cost
of binding).
8.2.
Thermodynamic Stability
ProteinMPNN-ddG selection ensures the designed sequence is thermodynamically stabilising
relative to the generated backbone. The PRODIGY binding energy gate corresponds to ap-
proximately sub-nanomolar binding affinity, placing designed binders in a clinically relevant
potency regime. MD stability over 1 nanosecond at 300 K further confirms that the predicted
complex does not dissociate or undergo large conformational rearrangements under physiological
simulation conditions.
8.3.
Escape Resistance
Three compounding mechanisms ensure escape resistance. First, hotspot prioritisation of con-
served residues via Shannon entropy scoring focuses design on functionally indispensable
positions. Second, DCA coupling enforcement engages co-constrained epistatic pairs, directly
quantified by the fDCA metric. Third, the fitness-coupled escape filter distinguishes genuine
threats from evolutionary dead ends by requiring both binding affinity reduction and viral fitness
tolerance.
8.4.
Manufacturability
Post-hoc classification in Phase 11 determines the manufacturing regime: solid-phase peptide
synthesis (SPPS) for peptides targeting ion channel blockers, E. coli expression for mini-proteins
and nanobodies targeting groove and receptor-binding interfaces, and mammalian expression for
larger proteins. Importantly, the geometry-derived length naturally falls in the most tractable
manufacturing regime for each target topology — a direct consequence of geometry-driven class
selection rather than a separate optimisation step.

9.
Use Case: LPLM Applied to the Influenza Virus
This section documents the application of LPLM to influenza A, which serves as the primary
biological test bed for the architecture described in this paper. Influenza presents four structurally
distinct therapeutic target interfaces, each with qualitatively different geometry, making it an
ideal validation substrate for the geometry-emergence principle. In all four cases, the binder
class was derived by the system without user specification of molecule type.
9.1.
The Influenza Target Landscape
Influenza A presents four principal therapeutic target interfaces, each demanding a different
binder class. The M2 channel pore, with an internal radius of approximately 5 Å, physically
excludes all molecules above a critical cross-sectional area — a geometry that directly implies
a short peptide binder. The hemagglutinin (HA) stem trimeric groove, spanning hundreds of
ångströms across two helices from different chains, requires a binder large enough to bridge

9.5.
M2 Ion Channel Pore — Geometry-Forced Class Selection
The M2 channel pore presents a cylindrical cavity of approximately 5 Å internal radius. LPLM’s
topology classifier assigns this as an ion channel pore topology, which maps to an implied binder
class of peptide (length 15–30 aa), the only class physically capable of penetrating the pore.
The key residues are His37 (proton relay, functionally indispensable with Shannon entropy of
approximately 0.03 across all sequenced influenza A strains) and Trp41 (gating residue, entropy
approximately 0.11). Both have conservation scores above 0.95, classifying them as core hotspots.
A minimum critical contact count of 2 is enforced, requiring the designed binder to contact
both His37 and Trp41. Because the pore geometry forces short peptides, the SDALO algorithm
operates in the 15–30 aa range; the geometric validation gate at this scale confirms that even 15
residues suffice to span the pore mouth (inter-hotspot distance approximately 11 Å), meaning
no lengths are eliminated and all candidates within the peptide class range are geometrically
feasible.
9.6.
Fitness-Coupled Escape Analysis — H274Y Example
The fitness-coupled escape evaluation is illustrated by the H274Y mutation in neuraminidase, a
variant historically associated with oseltamivir resistance. In some strain backgrounds, H274Y
carries a replication fitness cost of approximately 1.8 kcal/mol, which exceeds the 1.5 kcal/mol
threshold. Under the LPLM escape filter, this variant is therefore classified as a fitness-lethal
pseudo-escape: even if H274Y reduces binding affinity, the virus cannot sustain this mutation in
competition with wildtype strains, since it would be outcompeted under natural selection. As
a result, a binder that appears vulnerable in a fitness-agnostic screen against H274Y correctly
passes the fitness-coupled filter, and is classified as escape-resistant in the clinically relevant
sense.
9.7.
Summary of LPLM Performance on Influenza Targets
Across the four influenza targets, LPLM demonstrates the core principle of the architecture: each
target’s geometry deterministically implied its binder class, length range, and escape evaluation
strategy without any user input. The geometry-emergence principle held in all four topologically
distinct cases, confirming the architecture’s generality within the influenza target landscape and
motivating its extension to other RNA virus targets in future work.

10.
Discussion
10.1.
Limitations
The SDALO algorithm uses the 1.5 Å/residue helical stretch coefficient, calibrated for alpha-
helical geometry. For beta-strand binders, the rise per residue is approximately 3.5 Å, which
would impose a shorter minimum length for the same hotspot span. The current implementation
applies the helical coefficient uniformly; a topology-aware coefficient would improve accuracy
for receptor-binding site binders that adopt beta-scaffold geometries.
The fitness-coupled escape scoring relies on replication fitness cost estimates from the literature
or from deep mutational scanning experiments. For novel viral targets where such data is absent,
the system conservatively includes all escape variants, which may increase the false-positive
rejection rate.
Finally, molecular dynamics stability is evaluated over 1 nanosecond at 300 K. Longer sim-
ulations at microsecond scale would provide stronger thermodynamic evidence for complex
stability, though at substantially higher computational cost.
10.2.
Generalisation Beyond Influenza
The pipeline is target-agnostic at the architecture level. The topology-to-class map and the
SDALO algorithm apply to any protein-protein interface with defined groove geometry. In partic-
ular, the geometric validation gate is universally valid, since the hotspot span constraint holds for
any polypeptide chain regardless of target identity. SARS-CoV-2 spike protein interfaces, HIV
broadly neutralising antibody epitopes, and flavivirus envelope protein grooves are all candidates
for direct application of this architecture.
10.3.
LPLM in the Context of Modern Structural Biology AI
The field of AI for biology is currently transitioning from specialist single-task models toward
multi-modal systems. ESM3 (EvolutionaryScale, 2024) represents the most advanced current
attempt at a unified protein sequence-structure-function model, yet it does not include explicit
therapeutic design reasoning or escape resistance evaluation. LPLM occupies a complementary
position, operating as a system that reasons about the full chain from viral target physics to
therapeutic candidate, with the geometry-emergence principle encoded in the orchestration logic
rather than assumed as a pre-existing design choice.
An important implication of this architecture is the nature of the data it generates. Each LPLM
pipeline run produces structured tuples of target geometry, designed binder, complex structure,
and comprehensive score vectors — including gate-rejected negative examples — which consti-
tute a high-quality corpus for future standalone model development. Each gate applied by the
pipeline corresponds to a biophysical constraint that any downstream model must internalise.
This positions LPLM not only as a therapeutic design system, but as a principled data engine for
next-generation protein design research.

11.
Conclusion
We have presented LPLM, a computational architecture for de novo antiviral binder design that
operationalises the principle that therapeutic molecule class is a consequence of target interface
geometry rather than a design decision. The system orchestrates nine specialist AI models and
bioinformatic tools within a gate-controlled pipeline in which no binder class label is assigned
until after the designed molecule has been fully characterised.
The Sparse-to-Dense Adaptive Length Optimisation technique treats binder length as a contin-
uous optimisation variable derived from hotspot geometry, applied via a coarse-to-fine search
with pre-diffusion geometric validation gates. The supporting framework — Shannon entropy
conservation scoring, DCA with APC correction, fitness-coupled escape filtering, and multi-
signal composite scoring — provides a rigorous biophysical basis for every design decision in
the pipeline.
Applied to the influenza virus target suite, the architecture eliminated physically impossible
designs a priori, reducing invalid compute by 40 % and improving interface confidence metrics
by 13.8 % on the HA stem trimeric groove. Across all four influenza target topologies, binder
class and length were correctly derived from geometry without user specification, demonstrating
the generality and robustness of the approach. Beyond its immediate application to antiviral ther-
apeutics, LPLM is designed as a structured data engine whose outputs constitute a high-quality
corpus for training next-generation protein design systems that internalise the full biophysical
reasoning chain.
The correct binder for any viral target is not a design decision. It is a consequence
of the target’s physics. The role of the computational pipeline is not to choose the
binder class or its length — it is to compute the geometry of the target precisely
enough that both are implied by the computation.
